Summary

This course will provide a conceptual and practical understanding of how to leverage technologies in a range of common activities such as searching, social networking, presenting, and creating web content. The emphasis will be on using and understanding digital technologies in effective and ethical ways in our digital society. This is an undergraduate course that is a mix of lecture, discussion, tutorial and workshop.

This instance of the course (Winter 2013) will focus on Processing, an open-source programming language and development environment that is especially well-suited to creating interactive visual art and data visualizations. Considerable time and effort will be devoted to learning how to code. You need not have any technical background or programming experience, but you do need to be thoroughly committed to learning new skills that will require abundant perseverance and resourcefulness. The most important skills you will learn are not coding competencies, they're problem-solving competencies: how to figure out how to do what you don't know how to do in the process of creating digital works.

Despite the focus on Processing, this is not a programming course, it is a course about effective communication in a digital society. We are motivated by a desire to combine the traditional strengths of the humanities – critical thinking, effective expression, historical and cultural perspectives, nuanced and imaginative approaches, etc. –  with technical skills that will allow us to become creators of compelling digital content, going beyond more conventional academic forms of expression (like the essay).

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to

  • critically assess data visualizations (from news sources, blogs, advertising, etc.)
  • acquire, parse, filter and mine data
  • create and refine interactive data visualizations (in Processing)
  • develop effective strategies for learning new visualization techniques and solving problems
  • plan and implement a collaborative digital project
  • better understand and discuss the significance of data and data visualizations in our digital society

Course Materials

There is one required text: Ben Fry's Visualizing Data, which can be acquired in print and/or electronic formats from the publisher, or from other bookstores (the electronic files from the publisher are the most flexible (DRM-free), though there are cheaper editions available). This text is outstanding at describing processes for working on data visualizations, including several compelling examples that show useful techniques and concepts. However, the text isn't the most effective at teaching the fundamentals of the Processing language, but I will fill in gaps during class. As a further supplement, students may also want to consider purchasing the very economical Getting Started with Processing by Casey Reas & Ben Fry, again available from the publisher and elsewhere in print and electronic formats. Additional article-length readings will be assigned and made available through the course website.

(Very) Tentative Schedule

  • Week 1: intro to the course; getting started with Processing
  • Week 2: principles of data visualization; drawing in Processing
  • Week 3: epistemology of data; variables & arrays in Processing
  • Week 4: over-abundance of data; interactivity in Processing
  • Week 5: big data; loops and functions in Processing
  • Week 6: aesthetics of data; objects in Processing
  • Week 7: copyright & data; mapping in Processing
  • Week 8: privacy & data; time series in Processing
  • Week 9: ethics of data; acquiring data in Processing
  • Week 10: politics & data; parsing and cleaning data in Processing
  • Week 11: open data; networks in Processing
  • Week 12: hacking & data; connections & correlations in Processing
  • Week 13: data journalism; scatterplot maps in Processing
  • Week 14: student poster session; further directions with Processing (Java, Arduino, etc.)
